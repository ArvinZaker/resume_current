Brief Personal Essay 1
I have learned through growing up on multiple continents how socioe­conomic factors can affect individual health. In Iran, I joined a school project creating affordable insulin pumps, where I engaged deeply with individuals with financial diffic­ulties, learning about their daily struggle to choose between food and lifesaving medication. Seeing the impact of socioe­conomic factors on health sparked my resolve to address the unique needs of each community through culturally sensitive conver­sations and actions.

In University, COVID-19 provided me with such opportunity with Hands-on Health magazine. Recognizing a lack of accessible health information, I led a team of over 20 students to produce engaging healthcare articles. For each article, we dissem­inated and verified health information with medical profes­sionals and commun­icated it through concise articles to the student community, helping me learn how to organize collab­orative initiatives to address community needs.

As a next step, I joined Best Buddies to help connect students with individuals with disabi­lities. Working as a volunteer coordinator and later co-chair, we worked with community stakeh­olders like L'Arche and OCAPDD to identify those with pressing social needs. Moreover, through events, we promoted engagement among members to create a more inclusive environment. This experience showed me the importance of coordi­nating with key organi­zations to improve the community.

These experiences have shaped me into an individual committed to advancing health equity through culturally competent care and community action. In medicine, I aim to leverage my capabi­lities and multic­ultural perspective to take action toward inclusive healthcare for margin­alized communities across Canada.
Brief Personal Essay 2
Medicine is a highly dynamic field, with new revolu­tionary therapies appearing every day. Each one shifts healthcare, requiring one to destroy its old perception to stay up to date with current advanc­ements. Charlie encourages us to embrace this change, as the new ideas can elevate our skills and abilities.

One of the moments I saw this quote most clearly was during my thesis poster presen­tation. I presented my findings using a metric that measures paclitaxel drug response in cancer. However, when I explained the results to an attending physician, he questioned the utility of my entire project and all my prior work. Despite the initial shock, I took a deep breath and reminded myself it was the right time to hear the other side. When I asked the professor for further feedback, he pointed out that my method did not clearly define if a patient was sensitive to paclitaxel.

With this feedback and support, I spent several months deepening my unders­tanding of machine learning for drug response prediction. Rewriting all my code and paper was challenging, but I maintained motivation by reminding myself of the final goal of the revision.

All in all, the restru­cturing of my prior work after I received the vital feedback was a catalyst for my project, allowing me to develop xeMetron, which can identify drug-sensitive patients with 95% accuracy. This experience taught me to approach new advanc­ements openly, ready to challenge my views to improve patient care in my future medical career.

MD/​PhD - Research
Since November 2021, I have been fully dedicated to research, starting with cellular and molecular biology and moving on to bioinf­ormatics.

# Studying muscle fatigue kinetics

Supervisor: Dr. Jean-Marc Renaud

Institution: University of Ottawa

Year: 2020/​11 to 2022/​06

Title: Role of Adenosine Receptors on Mice Skeletal Muscle Fatigue Kinetics.
Under the mentorship of Dr. Jean-Marc Renaud, I embarked on my first research project exploring muscle fatigue mechanics. Previous studies showed that the presence of adenosine and adenosine receptors together can increase fatigue, but it was unknown if fatigue can be triggered by adenosine receptors alone. Our team hypoth­esized that altering adenosine receptor activity (A1/​A2A/​A2B/​A3) could influence fatigue. We tested this hypothesis by exposing isolated muscles to adenosine receptor agonists and antagonists and measuring the contractile force. We found out that modulating adenosine receptor activity did not impact the rate of fatigue. For this project, I performed muscle electr­ophysiology experiments and force measur­ements, leading to one poster at the UROP symposium.

# Study of the oncogenic effects of SMC5/​6 mutation

Supervisor: Dr. Arvind Mer (Primary Supervisor) and Dr. Damien D'Amours

Institution: Department of BMI, Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa

Year: 2022/​05 - 2022/​08

Title: Large-scale phenog­enomic analysis of human cancers uncovers frequent alterations affecting SMC5/​6 complex components in breast cancer
One of my initial bioinf­ormatics projects involved studying the oncogenic impact of the SMC5/​6 complex. SMC5/​6 complex is known as a regulator of chromosomal structure. However, no study has been conducted to analyze the functional impact of its mutation on genomic instability and oncogenesis. We hypoth­esized that SMC5/​6 mutations can be attributed to genomic instability and poor cancer outcomes. The first part of the study was performed by Dr. D'Amours' lab, where they analyzed the effects of inducing mutations in SMC5/​6 in yeast. They found that mutations in key SMC5/​6 functional domains lead to lower genomic instability. As such, in the second part, we assess the effect of SMC5/​6 mutation in humans. I collected and analyzed the prevalence and clinical signif­icance of the SMC5/​6 mutation using an aggregate of 64,959 patients and 199 cancer studies. We found that SMC5/​6 is frequently mutated and is associated with key markers of genomic instability: higher ploidy, lower survival, and cell cycle dysreg­ulation. Work from the project led to the journal article as a second author on NAR cancer.

# Assessing drug response prediction metrics in xenografts experiments
Supervisor: Dr. Arvind Mer

Institution: Department of BMI, Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa

Year: 2022/​09 - 2023/​08

Title: Comparative Analysis of Drug Response Metrics for Patient-Derived Xenograft

The longest project in Dr. Mer's lab has been developing tools to study drug response in patient-derived xenografts (PDX). In PDX, the patient tumor is grafted in mice, and the tumor volume is measured over time. Numerous methods exist to quantify the drug response of PDX; however, there is no consensus on the metric most suitable for this task, providing me with an exciting research opportunity.
To compare these metrics, I first conducted a literature review to identify the existing metrics and the xeMetron tool to compute these values for each PDX experiment. Next, I computed drug response metrics on PDX experiments with a range of replicates from 1 to 13 mice per condition. We identified that growth rate, gradient, and Brier score were the most capable of identifying drug responders even with one replicate.
My next objective was to study if PDX experiment results can be translated into clinical settings. I developed a new xeMetron metric to compute the probability of drug response and calculate the xeMetron value in clinical datasets. I found that patients with high xeMetron value had signif­icantly higher survival. Moreover, xeMetron could predict Paclitaxel sensitivity with 95% accuracy.The results of this project have led to two poster presen­tations, one oral presen­tation, and an honors thesis with a manuscript currently in preparation as a first author.

# Machine Learning and Text Mining in Drug Response Prediction
Supervisor: Dr. Arvind Mer

Institution: Department of BMI, Faculty of Medicine, University of Ottawa

Year: 2023/​09 - 2024/​02

Title: Text-mining-based feature selection for anticancer drug response prediction

To further advance my machine learning, I partic­ipated in a project to improve drug response prediction models using text mining. Text mining is a process where natural language processing is used to infer the strength of the relati­onship between a drug and a gene. An effective feature selection technique is necessary to develop a complete drug response prediction machine learning model. As such, we hypoth­esized that selecting features (genes) using literature text mining could be more effective than other methods. We created nine feature selection methods and trained a set of Elastic Net and Random Forest models using two pharma­cogenomic datasets. Comparing the performance of methods showed that the text-mining-based method performs better than the other methods. For this project, I contributed by programming and analyzing the results for six feature selection methods and visualizing the results. The work of the project led to a journal article as co-first author.

Through my work, I have gained a deep apprec­iation for the potential of bioinf­ormatics and comput­ational biology to transform clinical practice. By pursuing an MD/​PhD, I aim to continue bridging the gap between bench and bedside, using my research to inform clinical decision-making and improve patient outcomes.

MD/​PhD Statement of Intent
From my early years, a profound curiosity in science and technology guided my academic journey. In high school, with the encour­agement of my teachers, I started to read research articles which piqued my interest in learning about the scientific process. At the same time, the school created an opportunity for students to participate in a project with the guidance of a researcher. Our colleagues and I chose to study the development of an affordable insulin pump. Working with my colleagues and our guide, I spent countless hours learning about the research process and how they are structured in profes­sional settings to improve our unders­tanding of human biology. Using these methods, we prototype various delivery methods and presented our results at a local exhibition with great feedback.

Following this positive experience, I wanted to pursue biomedical research at the University. As such, in the second year, I joined Dr. Renaud's lab to explore muscle fatigue. To study the muscles, I had to learn to operate a wide range of tightly integrated electr­ophysiology equipment to stimulate and measure fatigue kinetics. After each experiment, the muscles were studied under a microscope, where we had a set of staining challenges. To resolve this issue, we system­atically examined and refined the protocol, and after months of trial and error, we resolved the problem by identifying the improper antibodies for our condition. This opportunity allowed me to learn the fundam­entals of experi­mental design and data analysis. More signif­icantly, I became interested in unders­tanding how knowledge from basic science research can be translated into actionable clinical results: the bench-to-bedside process.

To explore this process, I decided to explore transl­ational research, where scientists bridge the gap between basic science and clinical research leading to join Dr. Mer's lab. In the first project, I joined a project with a partner lab studying the impact of SMC5/​6 protein mutation on pan-cancer. This experience required extensive problem-solving skills to gather, store, and visualize the data of ~65000 patients. Due to the team-based nature of the work, we held frequent meetings to present findings and discuss future steps. Since the members of the partner lab specialized in cell biology, I had to learn to convey complex statistical findings accurately while being discernible for those with different expertise. With our collab­orative efforts, we obtained novel clinical insights on the impact of the SMC5/​6 mutation on cancer, leading to a publication in NAR Cancer. Since then, under the guidance of Dr. Mer, I have further pursued research focused on cancer and other disease pathologies, contri­buting to 4 public­ations to date.

My interest lies in person­alized medicine, where I can combine comput­ational tools and cellular biology to help establish a multi-omic framework for medical care. By pursuing an MD/​PhD, I can work under a broad network of researchers to facilitate bench-to-bedside translation, guide clinical decision-making, and improve treatment outcomes. Western University is a great fit for my interest as I can work under a broad network of researchers, like Dr. Kim and Dr. Shooshtari, allowing me to tackle more impactful research projects and facilitate the bench-to-bedside translation. Coupled with world-class facilities tailored for precision medicine research, such as Dr. Richard B. Kim's person­alized medicine clinic, Western University would be the ideal place for me to combine my interests and become an exemplary physician-scientist.
